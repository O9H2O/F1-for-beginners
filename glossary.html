Glossary

Every F1 term explained simply. Search for anything you hear during a race.

Apex
The innermost point of a corner — the geometric point where a driver clips closest to the inside edge of the track to minimise the cornering distance and maintain maximum speed.
Backmarker
A car running at the back of the field, typically a lap or more behind the leaders. Backmarkers must respect blue flags and let faster cars pass without impeding them.
Blistering
When a tyre overheats and develops blisters (bubbles) on its surface, causing chunks of rubber to detach. Severely affects grip and lap times.
Brake bias
A setting (adjusted via a dial on the steering wheel) that determines how braking force is distributed between the front and rear brakes. Drivers adjust this constantly during a lap.
Chequered flag
The black and white flag waved when the race leader completes the final lap, signalling the end of the race for all competitors.
Compound
The tyre formulation — how hard or soft the rubber is. Softer compounds provide more grip but wear faster. In F1, Pirelli supplies three compounds per race (hard, medium, soft), plus special intermediate and wet tyres.
Constructors' Championship
The title awarded to the team (constructor) that accumulates the most points across the season from both their drivers. Distinct from the Drivers' Championship.
DRS (Drag Reduction System)
A rear-wing flap that can be opened on designated straights when a driver is within one second of the car ahead, reducing aerodynamic drag and allowing a speed increase of around 20 km/h.
Downforce
Aerodynamic force pushing the car into the ground, generated by wings, diffusers, and bodywork. More downforce = more grip in corners, but also more drag on straights.
ERS (Energy Recovery System)
The hybrid component of the power unit that harvests energy from braking (MGU-K) and exhaust heat (MGU-H), storing it and deploying it for extra power.
FIA
Fédération Internationale de l'Automobile — the governing body of world motorsport, responsible for setting regulations, stewarding races, and enforcing the rules of F1.
Formation lap
The lap before the race start where cars drive around the circuit at reduced speed before forming up on the grid again for the standing start.
Grand Prix
French for "Grand Prize." Each individual F1 race is called a Grand Prix. The term predates F1, used in motor racing since the early 20th century.
Graining
When small pieces of rubber from a tyre tear off and stick back to the tyre surface, creating an uneven, grippy-then-slippy texture that makes the car unstable. Usually resolves itself as the tyre heat cycles.
HANS Device
Head And Neck Support — a mandatory safety device worn by drivers that prevents excessive head and neck movement in a frontal impact. Introduced after Ayrton Senna's death in 1994 and made mandatory in 2003.
Installation lap
A slow, exploratory lap done at the start of a session to check the car's systems are working properly before setting flying lap times.
Lapped traffic
Cars that are running more than one full lap behind the race leader. They must yield to faster cars and are shown blue flags.
Marbles
Small balls of rubber that form off the racing line as tyres shed material. Driving on marbles drastically reduces grip, which is why drivers stay strictly on the racing line in qualifying.
MGU-K / MGU-H
Motor Generator Unit – Kinetic/Heat. The two electric motor components of the hybrid power unit. MGU-K harvests energy under braking; MGU-H from exhaust gases. In 2026, MGU-H is replaced by a larger MGU-K.
Overcut
A pit stop strategy where a driver pits later than their rival, staying out on fresh tyres and setting fast laps while the rival is in the pit lane — aiming to emerge ahead.
Parc fermé
A restricted area where cars are kept after qualifying, with minimal modifications allowed. Ensures teams cannot make major changes between qualifying and the race.
Pit wall
The area alongside the pit lane where team principals, engineers, and strategists sit to manage the race. Also refers to the "pit wall" message a driver receives from their team.
Podium
The top three finishing positions. The podium ceremony takes place after the race — drivers receive trophies and their national anthems are played.
Pole position
Starting from first on the grid, awarded to the driver who sets the fastest time in Q3 of qualifying. Considered one of the most prestigious achievements in a race weekend.
Power unit
F1's term for the complete drivetrain package: the internal combustion engine plus all hybrid and energy recovery components. Not just called an "engine" because of the hybrid complexity.
Q1, Q2, Q3
The three segments of qualifying. Q1 eliminates the slowest 5 drivers, Q2 eliminates the next slowest 5, and Q3 (the top 10) determines pole position. See the Rules page for full details.
Racing line
The optimal path around a circuit — the route that allows the highest average speed. Drivers follow the racing line to maximise cornering speed, using the full width of the track.
Safety Car (SC)
A Mercedes road car (or Aston Martin in some regions) that leads the field at reduced speed when conditions are dangerous. All overtaking is forbidden while the safety car is out.
Stint
The period between pit stops. A driver's "first stint" lasts from the race start until their first pit stop; their "second stint" from that stop to the next, and so on.
Tyre deg (degradation)
How quickly a tyre loses performance during a stint. High tyre deg means the tyre wears quickly and the car gets slower; low deg means the driver can push for longer.
Undercut
A strategy where a driver pits earlier than the car ahead, putting on fresh tyres to go significantly faster — then passing the rival when they eventually pit.
Virtual Safety Car (VSC)
A slower version of the safety car protocol, where all drivers must reduce speed to a specific delta time. Used for minor incidents where a full safety car isn't needed.
Wake / Dirty air
The turbulent aerodynamic disruption created behind a car at speed. Following in another car's dirty air reduces downforce and therefore grip, making overtaking harder — a major issue F1's regulations aim to reduce.
