Since 1950

The History of F1

From Fangio in the 1950s to Verstappen today — 75 years of the greatest racing on Earth.

1950
The World Championship Begins
The first ever Formula 1 World Championship race took place at Silverstone on 13 May 1950. Italian Giuseppe Farina won both the race and the inaugural championship, driving for Alfa Romeo. Six races in Europe plus the Indianapolis 500 made up the first season.
1950s
The Fangio Era
Argentine driver Juan Manuel Fangio dominated the early years of the sport, winning five World Championships between 1951 and 1957 — a record that stood for 45 years. He raced for Alfa Romeo, Maserati, Ferrari, and Mercedes, and is still considered by many as the greatest racing driver of all time.
1960s
The British Teams Take Over
British constructors — particularly Lotus, Cooper, and BRM — began dominating. The rear-engined car revolution, pioneered by Cooper, changed the sport forever. Jim Clark, Jack Brabham, and Graham Hill traded championships through an era defined by incredible bravery and tragically high danger.
1970s
The Sponsorship Era
Commercial sponsorship transformed F1's look and finances in the 1970s. Tyrrell, Lotus, and Ferrari battled through the decade as Niki Lauda survived a near-fatal accident at the 1976 German Grand Prix, returned just six weeks later, and lost the championship to James Hunt by a single point in one of sport's greatest stories.
1980s
The Turbo Wars
Turbo-charged engines arrived and transformed performance — 1,000+ horsepower in qualifying trim. Renault, Ferrari, McLaren, and Williams fought a technological arms race. Alain Prost and Niki Lauda were McLaren team-mates in 1984, with Prost losing the championship by half a point in one of the most controversial finishes in history.
1988–1991
Senna vs Prost
The greatest rivalry in F1 history. Ayrton Senna (Brazil) and Alain Prost (France) were McLaren team-mates in 1988–89, and the tension boiled over into deliberate collisions at back-to-back Japanese Grands Prix. Senna was mystical and emotional; Prost was "The Professor" — calculated and technically brilliant. Four championships between them in four years.
1994
The Death of Senna and Safety Revolution
The 1994 San Marino Grand Prix weekend at Imola was the darkest in F1 history. Roland Ratzenberger died in qualifying; Ayrton Senna — widely considered the greatest of all time — died during the race. The tragedy triggered the biggest safety revolution in motorsport history. The FIA completely overhauled regulations, introduced mandatory crash testing, HANS devices, and much greater cockpit protection.
1994–2004
Schumacher and Ferrari's Dynasty
Michael Schumacher won two championships with Benetton (1994, 1995) then joined Ferrari for a decade-long project that delivered five consecutive world titles from 2000 to 2004. His 91 race victories (at the time, a seemingly unbreakable record) and seven championships defined an era of German engineering and absolute dominance.
2005–2008
New Champions, New Rivalry
Fernando Alonso became the youngest champion ever in 2005 at 24. A golden generation arrived: Hamilton made his debut in 2007 and lost the championship on the final lap of the final race by one point. In 2008, he won the title — again on the final lap of the final race — with a move that is simply known as "the pass at Turn 1, Brazil."
2009–2013
Red Bull and Vettel's Four Titles
A new team, Red Bull Racing, burst onto the scene with designer Adrian Newey creating a series of technically brilliant cars. Sebastian Vettel — who had given Toro Rosso their only win at age 21 in 2008 — won four consecutive championships from 2010 to 2013. His 2011 season (15 wins from 19 races) was considered historically dominant.
2014–2020
The Mercedes Hybrid Era
New hybrid power unit regulations in 2014 began the most dominant run in constructors' history. Mercedes won seven consecutive Constructors' Championships. Lewis Hamilton won six of his seven world titles during this period. His only blemish: 2016, when team-mate Nico Rosberg won the championship — then immediately retired.
2021
The Greatest Championship Battle
Max Verstappen vs Lewis Hamilton. Red Bull vs Mercedes. 22 races, lead changes all season, controversy at every corner. The final lap of the Abu Dhabi Grand Prix — a safety car period that allowed Verstappen to overtake Hamilton for the title — remains the most controversial moment in recent F1 history. Verstappen was champion by a single point.
2022–2024
The Verstappen Dynasty
New technical regulations in 2022 were supposed to close the field. Instead, Verstappen and Red Bull found an advantage and proceeded to win three more consecutive championships. The 2023 season was the most dominant individual performance in history: 19 wins from 22 races. By 2024, McLaren had finally closed the gap, winning the Constructors' Championship with Lando Norris as their star.
2026
A New Era Begins
The most significant technical regulation change since 2014. New power units where electrical motors provide around 50% of total power. New aerodynamic rules to improve wheel-to-wheel racing. Audi enters as a full works constructor. Lewis Hamilton moves to Ferrari. A new era that could — for the first time in a decade — genuinely reset the competitive order.
