Getting Started

How to Watch

Where to find F1, how to follow along, and how to make the most of every race weekend.

TV Broadcasters

United States

ESPN broadcasts every race, qualifying, and practice session live in the US. You can access it through most cable/satellite packages and streaming services like Sling TV, YouTube TV, Hulu Live, or DirecTV Stream.

United Kingdom

Sky Sports F1 has exclusive live coverage of every session in the UK. Channel 4 shows selected race highlights and some live races. Sky Sports requires a subscription — available via Sky, Now TV, or Sky Glass.

Canada

TSN / RDS covers F1 in Canada. Available through most cable providers or TSN Direct streaming.

Australia

Fox Sports (pay TV) and Network 10 (free-to-air, selected races) broadcast F1 in Australia.

Rest of World

Coverage varies significantly by country. Check your local sports broadcaster — F1 has broadcast deals covering over 180 countries.

F1 TV

F1 TV Pro is the sport's official streaming service. It offers live coverage of every session globally (subject to local broadcast restrictions), onboard cameras from every car, team radio, live timing, and a huge archive of historical races. It's the best way to watch F1 if you want complete access.

F1 TV Access is a cheaper tier that includes timing data, radio, and some content — but not live race streams in all markets.

F1 TV is available at f1.com/f1-tv — pricing varies by region.

Understanding a Race Broadcast

F1 broadcasts can feel overwhelming at first. Here's what to focus on:

  • The timing tower (usually on screen left or right) shows current race positions and gaps.
  • Purple/green/yellow sector times — Purple means fastest in that sector of all time in the session; green is driver's personal best; yellow is slower than personal best.
  • Tyre graphics — Shows what compound each driver is on and how many laps they've done on it.
  • DRS indicators — Shows when and where DRS is available.
  • Safety Car periods — The field bunches up; no overtaking, racing resumes after the safety car enters the pit lane.

Race Weekends: When to Watch

If you can only watch one session, watch the race. If you want more, watch qualifying on Saturday — it's pure drama, drivers pushing for absolute maximum lap time. Practice sessions are mainly for fans who want to understand strategy and technical developments.

Races typically start at 2pm or 3pm local time — which means they span from early morning to mid-afternoon US Eastern Time for European and Asian races, and afternoon/evening for Americas races.

Apps and Second Screen

The official F1 app is excellent as a companion during races. It shows live timing, driver tracking, tyre data, team radio (slight delay), and race control messages. Available on iOS and Android — free with basic features, subscription for full live timing.

Reddit's r/formula1 community is hugely active and a great place to discuss races in real time. Just be careful of spoilers if you're watching on delay!

Getting Into the Rhythm

F1 races about every two weeks through the season (roughly March to December). The schedule can seem intense at first — but you don't need to watch everything. Many fans watch the race live and catch qualifying highlights later, or vice versa.

If you're just starting out, pick a driver to support (or a team), learn who their main rivals are, and follow that story through the season. The sport becomes much more engaging when you have someone to root for.
