The Basics

How F1 Works

Formula 1 can seem complicated at first. Here's everything you need to know, explained simply.

The Basics

Formula 1 is the pinnacle of motorsport — the highest class of single-seater racing in the world. Twenty drivers compete across a season of around 24 races, called Grands Prix, held at circuits around the world.

There are two championships running simultaneously: the Drivers' Championship (individual drivers) and the Constructors' Championship (teams). Both use a points system based on finishing positions.

The Cars

F1 cars are the fastest racing cars in the world. They can accelerate from 0–100 km/h (0–60 mph) in under 2 seconds, reach top speeds of over 350 km/h (220 mph), and generate so much aerodynamic downforce that they could theoretically drive upside down on a ceiling.

Each team designs and builds their own car — which is why some cars are much faster than others. The cars must comply with strict regulations set by the FIA (the sport's governing body) on dimensions, weight, safety structures, and technical specifications.

In 2026, new regulations introduce hybrid power units where electrical energy provides around 50% of total power — making F1 cars greener and faster than ever before.

A Race Weekend

A typical F1 race weekend spans three days:

  • Friday: Two practice sessions (FP1 and FP2). Drivers learn the circuit, test setups, and gather data. Times don't count for anything.
  • Saturday: A final practice session (FP3), then Qualifying. Qualifying determines the grid order for Sunday's race — the fastest qualifier starts from "pole position" at the front.
  • Sunday: Race day. Drivers complete a set number of laps (enough to cover at least 305 km), with points awarded to the top 10 finishers.

Some weekends use a "Sprint" format, replacing FP2 with a shorter sprint race on Saturday that also awards points.

Qualifying

Qualifying is split into three segments — Q1, Q2, and Q3:

  • Q1 (18 minutes): All 20 drivers compete. The five slowest are eliminated and start the race from positions 16–20.
  • Q2 (15 minutes): The remaining 15 drivers compete. The five slowest are eliminated and start from positions 11–15.
  • Q3 (12 minutes): The top 10 battle for pole position. The fastest lap secures pole — the most prestigious qualifying achievement in racing.

Pit Stops

Tires are a crucial strategic element. F1 tires wear out during a race — and teams must use at least two different tyre compounds during a race. This means every driver must pit at least once.

A modern F1 pit stop takes as little as 1.8 seconds — with 20 mechanics simultaneously changing all four tires, checking the car, and sending it back out.

Strategy teams spend the race watching tire wear, track position, and gaps to opponents — deciding exactly when to pit each driver for maximum advantage.

Flags

  • Chequered flag 🏁 — Race is over.
  • Yellow flag — Hazard on track. No overtaking, slow down.
  • Red flag — Race stopped. Return to pit lane.
  • Blue flag — A faster car (usually a lap ahead) is approaching. Let them past.
  • Black and white flag — Warning for unsporting behaviour.
  • Black flag — Disqualification. Return to pit lane immediately.
  • Safety Car board / VSC — Safety car deployed, or Virtual Safety Car. Field bunches up, no overtaking.

DRS (Drag Reduction System)

DRS is an overtaking aid. When a driver is within one second of the car ahead at a designated "DRS detection point," they can open a flap on their rear wing on the following straight. This reduces drag and adds up to 20–25 km/h of top speed, making overtaking much more feasible.

DRS cannot be used in the first two laps, or in wet/safety car conditions.

Penalties

The stewards (race officials) can issue various penalties:

  • 5-second penalty — Added to the driver's race time at the end.
  • 10-second penalty — More serious offence.
  • Drive-through penalty — Driver must pass through the pit lane without stopping.
  • Stop-and-go penalty — Driver must stop in the pit box for 10 seconds.
  • Grid penalty — Applied to next race if issued after the race.
  • Disqualification — For the most serious violations (illegal car, ignoring black flag).
